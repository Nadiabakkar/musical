Uzbekistan

Uzbekistan’s music is a vibrant expression of its multicultural history—an audible legacy shaped by nomadic traditions, Islamic culture, and centuries of exchange along the Silk Road. From the spiritual elegance of Shashmaqam to regional folk styles and epic storytelling performed by bakhshi, music in Uzbekistan serves not just as entertainment, but as a carrier of memory, identity, and social values. In this project, we turn to digital tools to explore how this musical heritage is structured, connected, and represented in online knowledge spaces. Using SPARQL queries, Wikidata, and large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, we analyze how musical entities—artists, instruments, genres, and traditions—are documented and interlinked. These methods allow us to assess the visibility and completeness of Uzbek music in digital ecosystems, and to reflect on the challenges of modeling rich cultural traditions within structured data and AI outputs.

🔍 Detecting the Gap:

My analysis of Uzbek music data within Wikidata uncovered significant gaps, painting an incomplete picture of the nation's rich and diverse musical heritage. While a good number of Uzbek musicians were identified, the retrieved information skewed heavily towards older generations or less prominent figures. There's a notable absence of contemporary and emerging artists who are currently shaping the Uzbek music scene, indicating a crucial need for more up-to-date and comprehensive data on actively contributing musicians. A particularly striking void was observed in the documentation of fundamental musical elements like genres and record labels. Queries for these categories yielded very limited results, suggesting a substantial lack of structured data that would help contextualize Uzbek music within its stylistic classifications and industry frameworks. Furthermore, despite the visible growth of hip-hop in Uzbekistan, the query for Uzbek rappers returned a surprisingly low number of results, indicating a significant underrepresentation of this popular contemporary genre. Perhaps the most alarming discovery was the complete absence of Uzbek Traditional/Folk Songs and Albums in the query results. This is a profound omission, considering that traditional and folk music forms the very bedrock of Uzbekistan's cultural identity, encompassing centuries of historical and artistic expression. This void underscores a critical failure in digitally preserving and making accessible a cornerstone of Uzbek musical heritage. Collectively, these gaps highlight that despite its importance, Uzbek music remains significantly marginalized in digital knowledge repositories, pointing to a pressing need for more inclusive and accurate documentation.
